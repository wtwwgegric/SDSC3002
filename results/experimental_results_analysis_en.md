# Experimental Results and Analysis

We evaluated three retrieval strategies for item-based collaborative filtering under a binary-preference setting with Jaccard similarity: a naive brute-force baseline, an optimized sparse exact baseline, and an approximate MinHash+LSH pipeline. All comparisons used the same top-k setting ($k = 10$), with 100 MinHash functions and 50 LSH bands (2 rows per band). For fairness, the three methods were always tested on the same sampled item subset at each subset size.

On MovieLens 1M, the three-tier comparison reveals a clear runtime crossover. The naive brute-force baseline scales quadratically and becomes inefficient quickly, while the sparse exact baseline remains strong because sparse matrix multiplication exploits the structure of the user-item interactions. Nevertheless, as the subset size increases, the LSH pipeline gradually amortizes its signature-generation and candidate-verification overhead. In our experiments, the runtime crossover between LSH and the sparse exact baseline occurs at roughly 900 to 1000 items, and from 1200 items onward the LSH advantage becomes stable. For example, at 1000 items, LSH requires 0.200 seconds versus 0.231 seconds for the sparse exact baseline, and at 3000 items, LSH reduces runtime to 0.975 seconds compared with 2.099 seconds for sparse exact and 6.491 seconds for naive brute-force. This corresponds to a 2.15x speedup over sparse exact and a 6.66x speedup over naive brute-force, with recall@10 of 0.565.

The Amazon Musical Instruments 5-core dataset shows a different and more informative pattern. Because the data are much sparser, the optimized sparse exact baseline becomes exceptionally strong: the number of item pairs with nonzero overlap remains only a tiny fraction of the theoretical all-pairs search space, so exact computation can skip most useless comparisons very efficiently. Under the same LSH configuration, we observed no runtime crossover at all, even when scaling to the full dataset. At 5000 items, the sparse exact baseline takes only 0.169 seconds, whereas LSH requires 0.647 seconds. On the full 10,606-item dataset, sparse exact still finishes in 0.665 seconds, while LSH takes 1.814 seconds and achieves recall@10 of only 0.272. Although LSH is still much faster than the naive brute-force baseline on Amazon, it never surpasses the sparse exact implementation in our measured range.

These results lead to two main conclusions. First, the naive brute-force baseline is useful as a conceptual reference, but it is not competitive once the item set becomes moderately large. Second, whether LSH is practically beneficial depends heavily on data characteristics and on the strength of the exact baseline. On MovieLens 1M, LSH becomes worthwhile after the subset size passes the runtime crossover region, offering a meaningful speed-accuracy tradeoff. On Amazon, however, the extreme sparsity makes the exact sparse method so effective that LSH cannot recover its additional overhead. Therefore, the value of LSH in recommender systems should not be framed as universally outperforming exact search; rather, it should be understood as a scalability technique whose benefit appears only when the candidate reduction is large enough to outweigh approximation overhead.

For presentation in the report, the most useful figures are the runtime-versus-subset-size plot, the speedup-versus-subset-size plot, and the LSH quality plot that jointly shows recall@10 and candidate ratio. Together, these figures make the central message visually clear: LSH can outperform brute-force search early and can eventually beat an optimized exact baseline on some datasets, but highly sparse datasets may allow exact sparse methods to remain superior even at full scale.
